Soil Mobility and the Ecological Afterlives
This study emerges from a critical engagement with debates that reconceptualize urban soil not as a stable ground, but as a dynamic, relational, and metabolically active medium. In Ankara, the large-scale excavation processes associated with the construction of Bilkent City Hospital have displaced and redistributed significant volumes of soil — transferring not only material, but embedded biological, chemical, and hydrological properties. At Beytepe Pond, the emergence of wetland vegetation in an area expected to exhibit steppe characteristics suggests that transported soils carry latent ecological potentials activated under new environmental conditions.

The methodology also incorporates territorial relationality — a multi-scalar reading of the Bilkent area as a relational territory in which soil movements, hydrological systems, and ecological formations are understood not in isolation but as expressions of a connected fluvial and geological structure. The stream system constituting the fluvial infrastructure of Ankara — from Ankara Çubuk Çayı to İmrahor Deresi and beyond — provides the territorial frame within which the displacement of soil from the Bilkent excavation zone must be read.
Click image to zoom

The stream system that constitute the fluvial structure of Ankara

The movement of soil through excavation practices produces new assemblages between ground, water, and vegetation, destabilizing conventional distinctions between natural and artificial environments. Rather than treating emergent formations as anomalies, this study interprets them as indicators of a disrupted yet generative soil metabolism.

Transported soils are not inert material — they carry traces of their origin: microbial communities, clay mineral compositions, hydrological memory, and seed banks. When deposited in a new location, these properties become latent, awaiting the conditions for reactivation.

This reappearance of ecological relations and histories embedded within soil — now made visible in a different spatial context — is understood here as an ecological afterlife: the continuation of a soil system's metabolic and biological capacities beyond its original site.

The study is guided by the following central question: How does the mobility of urban soil reconfigure new ecological conditions and generate unexpected habitats?

In the case of partial observation — Beytepe Pond, Bilkent City Hospital, and the Arboretum — do the emergent plant formations prove the erasure of the steppe, or do they reveal the soil's capacity to retain and rearticulate its own ecological archive in a new location?

Through these questions, the study repositions soil not as a passive substrate to be stabilized, but as an active agent whose circulation reshapes urban ecologies, produces new forms of habitability, and simultaneously carries and reactivates traces of past environments.

Theoretical Frame

Key Perspectives

The research draws on five theoretical perspectives that collectively reframe urban soil as a dynamic, relational, and politically charged medium — moving beyond instrumentalized understandings of earth as mere substrate.

Meulemans
(2020)
Soil as Produced
Urban soil is not found but made — continuously produced through human intervention, infrastructure, and material displacement. Urban pedogenesis as political process.
Clark
(2016)
Stratified & Political
Soil is stratified and politically charged — its layers encode histories of power, land use, and ecological violence across time and space.
McNeill
(2020)
Volumetric Governance
Soil is governed volumetrically — its management is a three-dimensional spatial and political practice with deep ecological consequences.
Harris
(2025)
Metabolic Circulation
Soil metabolically circulates through urban systems. Road incision and excavation displace soil strata, redirect subsurface water flows, and set in motion a supply of surplus soil.
Puig de la Bellacasa
(2014)
Living Infrastructure / Care
Soil is a living infrastructure requiring care — its health and vitality depend on sustained relational and political attention beyond instrumental use.
04

Methodology

Multi-scalar & Interpretive Approach

At the core of the study is the tracing of soil movement generated through large-scale excavation at Bilkent City Hospital. Spatial mapping techniques and site analysis identify the points of extraction, transportation, and deposition of soil volumes across the territory.

Temporal analysis, supported by satellite imagery and planning documents, is used to understand how these movements unfold over time and how they correlate with changes in land use and ecological conditions.

The methodology combines spatial mapping, field observation, territorial relationality, experimental orthographic projections, and critical material interpretation through plant species analysis.

Rather than establishing linear causality between soil movement and ecological change, the study employs an interpretive and relational approach that foregrounds connections between excavation, hydrological systems, and emergent vegetation.

Field observations at Beytepe Pond provide a situated understanding of soil displacement processes. Particular attention is given to soil composition, surface conditions, moisture patterns, and the emergence of vegetation in areas where such ecologies would not typically occur.

These observations are not treated as isolated data points, but as indicators of broader material and ecological transformations — each plant species a trace of the soil's origin, composition, and metabolic state.

The Botanical Park's unfinished entry and the Arboretum are targeted as the tertiary observation zone. Uncompleted construction and vegetation processes have resulted in a condition of wilderness remaining at the center of a designed area.

This condition reflects the ever-changing status of existing ecological particles and the bare threshold between refurbishment and abandonment — a site where design intention and metabolic process are in negotiation.

The methodology incorporates a material reading of soil as a stratified and relational entity. By examining the physical characteristics and concealed water features within the ground, the study interprets soil as both a carrier of past conditions and a medium through which new ecological assemblages emerge.

Complemented by critical reading of planning documents, archival research, aerial photographs, and site visits — revealing how soil is differently framed across institutional, ecological, and everyday discourses. Soil is understood not only as material, but as archive: retaining, transporting, and rearticulating ecological traces across urban space.

07

Conceptual Framework

Soil as Process — Displaced Volume

Soil is best understood as a process rather than as an object (Meulemans, 2020). Its status as inert or lively shifts across strata and over time — neither purely substrate nor purely ecological agent. Habitat reproduces soil — the cycle of socioecological metabolism continues.

01

Soil Movement

Road incision and building excavation displace soil strata, redirect subsurface water flows, and set in motion a supply of surplus soil (Harris, 2025). The physical displacement of earth from the Bilkent City Hospital excavation zone transfers embedded biological, chemical, and hydrological properties — initiating processes that cannot be fully anticipated or controlled.

02

Soil Metabolism

Clay–water–organic matter cycle. Microbial activity, calcium and carbon flows. Soil alternates between lively and inert in response to disturbance and capital demand (Harris, 2025). The biological and chemical processes within displaced soil layers persist through displacement — the soil metabolizes in its new location, producing new assemblages between ground, water, and vegetation.

03

Habitat

All strata form a single interconnected living system. Fusing abiotic and biotic elements in ways that cannot be reduced to their component parts (Harris, 2025). The emergent ecologies at Beytepe Pond, the Arboretum, and the Botanical Park are ecological afterlives: Phragmites australis and Lamium purpureum are not opportunists — they are reading the soil's memory.

Central Proposition

Soil is not a passive substrate to be stabilized — it is an active agent whose circulation reshapes urban ecologies, produces new forms of habitability, and simultaneously carries and reactivates traces of past environments.
