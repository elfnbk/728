The Question

Research Framework
How does soil
metabolize urban
transformation?

The site around 6002. Cadde is not merely a backdrop to urban development — it is an active participant. Soils accumulate, shift, and respond; they carry the trace of every disturbance while sustaining the conditions for ecological recovery.

This research asks: what can the soil tell us about the history and future of a landscape? How do species act as indicators of soil health? And how might design work with — rather than against — the metabolic processes already underway?

The investigation moves between scales: from the molecular chemistry of clay particles to the territorial pattern of road infrastructure; from monthly satellite readings to the lifespan of a reed colony along the Beytepe shoreline.

Habitat Strata / Soil as a Living System

Aerial / Canopy Layer

The aerial layer comprises the above-ground biomass — primarily reed stands along water channels and shrub canopy at habitat margins. This layer moderates microclimate, captures airborne particles, and provides nesting structures for fauna. On disturbed sites, its absence marks zones of maximum ecological stress.

Timelapse

Transformation 2004 — 2025

Twenty-one years of satellite imagery — from pre-development greenfield (2004) to the layered infrastructure landscape of today (2025). Three scales of reading: every five years for the long arc; monthly from 2014–2015 and 2017–2019 for the construction period; and four key comparison frames across the site.

Phase I
2004–10
Ground Zero

Predominantly agricultural and semi-natural landscape. Early traces of road alignment visible. Alluvial and native soil systems largely intact. Beytepe Pond shoreline colonised by reed stands.

Phase II
2012–19
Development of the Road

Intensive construction period. Monthly imagery from 2017–2019 shows the simultaneous development of road infrastructure and the movement of the surrounding environment. Imported fill soils introduced.

Phase III
2020–25
Aftermath

Post-construction ecological succession. Disturbed soils begin to be colonised. Drainage and water-soil dynamics reorganise around new topography. Species return to margins of the corridor.

Soil Movement

Soil Metabolism

Designing with biological and chemical processes: microbial cycles, clay mineral activity, capillary water dynamics. Soil as living infrastructure — responsive, layered, and continuous.

Species-led design strategies — using indicator plants to identify zones of ecological potential and structure a phased recovery landscape along the 6002. Cadde corridor and Beytepe Pond margins.

Soil as Process — Design as Response

The project proposes reading the site not as a problem to be solved but as a metabolic process to be understood and extended. Soil is the archive of past disturbance and the medium of future habitat. Design operates at the intersection of infrastructure, ecology, and time — working with what the land already knows how to do.
